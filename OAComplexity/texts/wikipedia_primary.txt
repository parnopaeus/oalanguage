<p><b>Open access</b> (<b>OA</b>) refers to <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> outputs which are distributed online and free of cost or other barriers,<sup id="cite_ref-suber_overview_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suber_overview-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and possibly with the addition of a <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_license" title="Creative Commons license">Creative Commons license</a> to promote reuse.<sup id="cite_ref-suber_overview_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suber_overview-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Open access can be applied to all forms of published research output, including <a href="/wiki/Peer-reviewed" class="mw-redirect" title="Peer-reviewed">peer-reviewed</a> and non peer-reviewed <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journal</a> articles, <a href="/wiki/Conference_papers" class="mw-redirect" title="Conference papers">conference papers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theses" class="mw-redirect" title="Theses">theses</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> book chapters,<sup id="cite_ref-suber_overview_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suber_overview-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">monographs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Academic articles (as historically seen in paper-based <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journals</a>) have been the main focus of the movement. Conventional (non-open access) journals cover publishing costs through <a href="/wiki/Subscription_business_model" title="Subscription business model">access tolls</a> such as subscriptions, site licenses or <a href="/wiki/Pay-per-view" title="Pay-per-view">pay-per-view</a> charges. Open access research is advanced by a range of distribution mechanisms and business models. These include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Self-archiving" title="Self-archiving">Self-archiving</a> - green: After peer review by a journal, the author posts the same content the journal will be publishing to a web site controlled by the author, the research institution that funded or hosted the work, or which has been set up as a central open access repository.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Open_access_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access journal">Open access journal</a>: The publisher of the journal makes all articles and related content available for free on the journal's web site.
<ul><li>Open access journal funded by <a href="/wiki/Article_processing_charge" title="Article processing charge">article processing charges</a> paid by authors or research sponsor - gold<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Open access journal funded by an <a href="/wiki/Academic_institution" title="Academic institution">academic institution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Learned_society" title="Learned society">learned society</a> or a government information center (no publication fees are paid by authors) - platinum,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> diamond,<sup id="cite_ref-fuchs2013_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuchs2013-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gaj_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaj-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or gold<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Delayed_open-access_journal" title="Delayed open-access journal">Delayed open-access journals</a> - provide open access after an <a href="/wiki/Embargo_(academic_publishing)" title="Embargo (academic publishing)">embargo</a> period, typically 6â€“12 months or longer<sup id="cite_ref-Suber2012_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hybrid_open_access_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Hybrid open access journal">Hybrid open access journals</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> at least partially funded by subscriptions, and only provide open access for those individual articles for which the authors (or research sponsor) pay a publication fee<sup id="cite_ref-Suber2012_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Advantages and disadvantages of open access have generated considerable discussion amongst researchers, academics, librarians, university administrators, funding agencies, government officials, commercial <a href="/wiki/Publisher" class="mw-redirect" title="Publisher">publishers</a>, editorial staff and <a href="/wiki/Learned_society" title="Learned society">society</a> publishers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Reactions of existing publishers to open access journal publishing have ranged from moving with enthusiasm to a new open access business model, to experiments with providing as much free or open access as possible, to active lobbying against open access proposals. There are many publishers that started up as open access-only  publishers, such as <a href="/wiki/Public_Library_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Library of Science">PLOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindawi_Publishing_Corporation" title="Hindawi Publishing Corporation">Hindawi Publishing Corporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frontiers_Media" title="Frontiers Media">Frontiers in...</a> journals, <a href="/wiki/MDPI" title="MDPI">MDPI</a> and <a href="/wiki/BioMed_Central" title="BioMed Central">BioMed Central</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gratis_and_libre_open_access">Gratis and libre open access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gratis and libre open access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In order to reflect actual practice in providing <a href="/wiki/Gratis_versus_libre" title="Gratis versus libre">two different types</a> of open access, the further distinction between gratis open access and libre open access was added in 2006 by the principal drafter of the original <a href="/wiki/Budapest_Open_Access_Initiative" title="Budapest Open Access Initiative">BOAI</a> definition.<sup id="cite_ref-Gratis_and_Libre_Open_Access_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gratis_and_Libre_Open_Access-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Gratis open access refers to online access free of charge, and libre open access refers to online access free of charge plus some additional re-use rights.<sup id="cite_ref-Gratis_and_Libre_Open_Access_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gratis_and_Libre_Open_Access-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Libre open access covers the kinds of open access defined in the <a href="/wiki/Budapest_Open_Access_Initiative" title="Budapest Open Access Initiative">Budapest Open Access Initiative</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bethesda_Statement_on_Open_Access_Publishing" title="Bethesda Statement on Open Access Publishing">Bethesda Statement on Open Access Publishing</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Declaration_on_Open_Access_to_Knowledge_in_the_Sciences_and_Humanities" title="Berlin Declaration on Open Access to Knowledge in the Sciences and Humanities">Berlin Declaration on Open Access to Knowledge in the Sciences and Humanities</a>. The re-use rights of libre OA are often specified by various specific <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_license" title="Creative Commons license">Creative Commons licenses</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Suber_2012_68â€“69_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber_2012_68â€“69-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> these almost all require <a href="/wiki/Attribution_(copyright)" title="Attribution (copyright)">attribution of authorship</a> to the original authors.<sup id="cite_ref-Gratis_and_Libre_Open_Access_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gratis_and_Libre_Open_Access-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Suber_statements_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber_statements-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_access_publishing">Open access publishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Open access publishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One option for authors who wish to make their work openly accessible is to publish in a journal or book that makes research output immediately available from the publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-linkinghub.elsevier.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linkinghub.elsevier.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This is sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Gold_open_access" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold open access">gold open access</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-suber_overview_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suber_overview-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are many financial models for open access publications.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Open access can be provided by commercial publishers, who may publish open access as well as subscription-based journals, or dedicated open-access publishers such as <a href="/wiki/Public_Library_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Library of Science">Public Library of Science</a> (PLOS) and <a href="/wiki/BioMed_Central" title="BioMed Central">BioMed Central</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_processing_charges">Article processing charges<span id="Author-pays_model"></span><span id="Fee-based"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Article processing charges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Article_processing_charge" title="Article processing charge">Article processing charge</a></div>
<p>In one model of open access, journals generate revenue by charging publication fees in order to make the work openly available at the time of publication.<sup id="cite_ref-Socha_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Socha-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fuchs2013_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuchs2013-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gaj_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaj-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The money might come from the author but more often comes from the author's <a href="/wiki/Research_grant" class="mw-redirect" title="Research grant">research grant</a> or employer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Some publishers will waive all or part of the fee for authors from <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">less developed economies</a>. Journals charging publication fees normally take various steps to ensure that <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer reviewers</a> do not know whether authors have requested, or been granted, fee waivers, or to ensure that every paper is approved by an independent editor with no financial stake in the journal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> While the payments are often incurred <i>per article published</i> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/BMC_journals" class="mw-redirect" title="BMC journals">BMC journals</a> or <a href="/wiki/PLOS_ONE" class="mw-redirect" title="PLOS ONE">PLOS ONE</a>), there are some journals that apply them <i>per manuscript submitted</i> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_Chemistry_and_Physics" title="Atmospheric Chemistry and Physics">Atmospheric Chemistry and Physics</a> until recently) or <i>per author</i> (<a href="/wiki/PeerJ" title="PeerJ">PeerJ</a>). As of June 2018, only 26% of journals in the <a href="/wiki/Directory_of_Open_Access_Journals" title="Directory of Open Access Journals">Directory of Open Access Journals</a> (DOAJ) required payment of article processing charges. A 2013 study showed this practice was higher in journals with a scientific or medical focus (43% and 47% respectively), and lowest in journals publishing in the arts and humanities (0% and 4% respectively).<sup id="cite_ref-Kozak&amp;Hartley_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kozak&amp;Hartley-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Traditionally, many academic journals levied page charges, long before open access became a possibility.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>There currently is a growing global debate<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> regarding open access's ideology and ethics and its related article processing charges, as they are being created and managed by academic journal and monograph publisher conglomerates together with some national and international academic institutions and government bodies. One controversy is "double dipping", where both authors and subscribers are charged.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Groups offering open access solutions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> include the Publishers for Development and Research4Life projects and activities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidized_open_access_publications">Subsidized open access publications<span id="No-fee"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subsidized open access publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>No-fee open access journals, also known as "platinum" or "diamond"<sup id="cite_ref-fuchs2013_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuchs2013-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gaj_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaj-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> have no fees for readers and no article processing charges or publication fees for authors.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> They use a variety of <a href="/wiki/Business_model" title="Business model">business models</a>. As summarized by Peter Suber:<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> "Some no-fee OA journals have direct or indirect subsidies from institutions like universities, laboratories, research centers, libraries, hospitals, museums, <a href="/wiki/Learned_society" title="Learned society">learned societies</a>, foundations, or government agencies. Some have revenue from a separate line of non-OA publications. Some have revenue from advertising, auxiliary services, membership dues, endowments, reprints, or a print or premium edition. Some rely, more than other journals, on volunteerism. Some undoubtedly use a combination of these means".
</p><p>Open access monographs are subsidized through a variety of means as well. <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_Unlatched" title="Knowledge Unlatched">Knowledge Unlatched</a> and unglue.it crowdsource funding in order to make a work available open access.<sup id="cite_ref-Montgomery_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montgomery-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_access_repositories">Open access repositories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Open access repositories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Self-archiving" title="Self-archiving">Self-archiving</a></div>
<p>Self-archiving, also known as green open access, refers to the practice of depositing articles in an <a href="/wiki/Open_access_repository" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access repository">open access repository</a>, where it can be accessed for free.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-greenandgold_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenandgold-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-roar_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roar-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DemystifyingOpenAccess_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DemystifyingOpenAccess-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Repositories may be specific to an <a href="/wiki/Institutional_repository" title="Institutional repository">institution</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Disciplinary_repository" title="Disciplinary repository">discipline</a> (e.g.<a href="/wiki/ArXiv" title="ArXiv">arXiv</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Scholarly_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Scholarly society">scholarly society</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Modern_Language_Association" title="Modern Language Association">MLA</a>'s CORE Repository), or a funder (e.g.<a href="/wiki/PubMed_Central" title="PubMed Central">PubMed Central</a>).
The <a href="/wiki/Open_Archives_Initiative" title="Open Archives Initiative">Open Archives Initiative</a> (OAI) lists 2937 conforming <a href="/wiki/Institutional_repository" title="Institutional repository">repositories</a>.
</p><p>Open access self-archiving was first formally proposed in 1994<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Stevan_Harnad" title="Stevan Harnad">Stevan Harnad</a> in his "<i><a href="/wiki/Subversive_Proposal" title="Subversive Proposal">Subversive Proposal</a></i>". However, self-archiving was already being done by computer scientists in their local <a href="/wiki/FTP" class="mw-redirect" title="FTP">FTP</a> archives in the 1980s,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> later harvested into <a href="/wiki/CiteSeer" class="mw-redirect" title="CiteSeer">CiteSeer</a>.
</p><p>Many publishers permit authors to self-archive in an open access repository, but may place restrictions on which version of the work may be shared and/or require an embargo period following the original date of publication. What is deposited can be either a <a href="/wiki/Preprint" title="Preprint">preprint</a>, or the peer-reviewed <a href="/wiki/Postprint" title="Postprint">postprint</a> â€“ either the author's refereed, revised final draft or the publisher's version of record. Some publishers require delays, or an <a href="/wiki/Embargo_(academic_publishing)" title="Embargo (academic publishing)">embargo</a>, on when a research output in a repository may be made open access.<sup id="cite_ref-Embargos_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Embargos-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> To find out if a publisher or journal has given a green light to author self-archiving, the author can check the Publisher Copyright Policies and Self-Archiving list<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/SHERPA/RoMEO" title="SHERPA/RoMEO">SHERPA/RoMEO</a> web site.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Searching_for_open-access_publications">Searching for open-access publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Searching for open-access publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">See also: the categories <a href="/wiki/Category:Scholarly_search_services" title="Category:Scholarly search services">Scholarly search services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Full_text_scholarly_online_databases" title="Category:Full text scholarly online databases">Full text scholarly online databases</a>.</div>
<p>Searching each open access repository individually is impractical. 
Open access articles can be found with a <a href="/wiki/Web_search" class="mw-redirect" title="Web search">web search</a>, using any general <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">search engine</a> or those specialized for the scholarly and scientific literature, such as: <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">Google Scholar</a>, <a href="/wiki/OAIster" title="OAIster">OAIster</a>, 
<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://openaccess.xyz/about.html">openaccess.xyz</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.base-search.net/about/en/">base-search.net</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-base-search_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-base-search-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://core.ac.uk/search/">core.ac.uk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many open-access repositories offer a programmable interface to query their content.
Some of them use a generic protocol, such as <a href="/wiki/Protocol_for_Metadata_Harvesting" class="mw-redirect" title="Protocol for Metadata Harvesting">OAI-PMH</a> (eg <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.base-search.net/about/en/">base-search.net</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-base-search_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-base-search-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://api.archives-ouvertes.fr/docs/oai">HAL</a>).
In addition, some repositories propose a specific API, such as the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://arxiv.org/help/api/index">arXiv API</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://dev.dissem.in/api.html">Dissemin API</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://unpaywall.org/products/api">Unpaywall/oadoi API</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.base-search.net/about/download/base_interface.pdf">base-search API</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal#Electronic_publishing" title="Scientific journal">Scientific journal Â§&#160;Electronic publishing</a></div>
<p>Like the self-archived green open access articles, most gold open access journal articles are distributed via the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-suber_overview_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suber_overview-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> due to low distribution costs, increasing reach, speed, and increasing importance for scholarly communication. <a href="/wiki/Open_source_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source software">Open source software</a> is sometimes used for <a href="/wiki/Open_access_repositories" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access repositories">open access repositories</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BOAI_FAQ_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOAI_FAQ-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Category:Publication_management_software" title="Category:Publication management software">open access journal websites</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and other aspects of open access provision and open access publishing.
</p><p>Access to online content requires Internet access, and this distributional consideration presents physical and sometimes financial barriers to access. Proponents of open access argue that Internet access barriers are relatively low in many circumstances, that efforts should be made to subsidize universal Internet access, whereas pay-for-access presents a relatively high additional barrier over and above Internet access itself.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>There are various open access aggregators that list open access journals or articles. ROAD<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> (the Directory of Open Access scholarly Resources) synthesizes information about open access journals and is a subset of the <a href="/wiki/ISSN" class="mw-redirect" title="ISSN">ISSN</a> register. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=OALibrary&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="OALibrary (page does not exist)">OALibrary</a> provides open and free access to a large database of scientific research papers, covering all topics.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Users may browse to find open access journals by country or by subject. <a href="/wiki/SHERPA/RoMEO" title="SHERPA/RoMEO">SHERPA/RoMEO</a> lists international publishers that allow the published version of articles to be deposited in <a href="/wiki/Institutional_repositories" class="mw-redirect" title="Institutional repositories">institutional repositories</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Directory_of_Open_Access_Journals" title="Directory of Open Access Journals">Directory of Open Access Journals</a> (DOAJ) contains over 8,000 peer-reviewed open access journals of varying open access policies for searching and browsing<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1998, several universities founded the <a href="/wiki/Public_Knowledge_Project" title="Public Knowledge Project">Public Knowledge Project</a> to foster open access, and developed the open-source journal publishing system <a href="/wiki/Open_Journal_Systems" title="Open Journal Systems">Open Journal Systems</a>, among other scholarly software projects. As of 2010, it was being used by approximately 5,000 journals worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several initiatives provide an alternative to the American and English language dominance of existing publication indexing systems, including <a href="/wiki/Index_Copernicus" title="Index Copernicus">Index Copernicus</a> (Polish), <a href="/wiki/SciELO" title="SciELO">SciELO</a> (Portuguese, Spanish) and <a href="/wiki/Redalyc" title="Redalyc">Redalyc</a> (Spanish).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies_and_mandates">Policies and mandates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Policies and mandates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Open_access_mandate" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access mandate">Open access mandate</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="#Research_funders_and_universities"> Â§&#160;Research funders and universities</a></div>
<p>Many universities, research institutions and research funders have adopted mandates requiring their researchers to provide open access to their <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer-reviewed</a> research articles by <a href="/wiki/Self-archiving" title="Self-archiving">self-archiving</a> them in an open access repository.<sup id="cite_ref-roarmap_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roarmap-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Research Councils UK spent nearly Â£60m on supporting their open access mandate between 2013 and 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Some publishers and publisher associations have lobbied against introducing mandates.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The idea of mandating self-archiving was mooted at least as early as 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Since 2003<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> efforts have been focused on open access mandating by the funders of research: governments,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> research funding agencies,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and universities.<sup id="cite_ref-roarmap_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roarmap-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Registry_of_Open_Access_Repository_Mandates_and_Policies" class="mw-redirect" title="Registry of Open Access Repository Mandates and Policies">Registry of Open Access Repository Mandates and Policies</a> (ROARMAP) is a searchable international database charting the growth of <a href="/wiki/Open_access_mandate" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access mandate">open access mandates</a>. As of December 2017, mandates have been registered by over 600 universities (including Harvard, MIT, Stanford, University College London, and University of Edinburgh) and over 100 research funders worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-roar_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roar-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivations">Motivations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Motivations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal_publishing_reform" title="Academic journal publishing reform">Academic journal publishing reform</a></div>
<p>Open access itself (mostly green and gratis) began to be sought and provided worldwide by researchers when the possibility itself was opened by the advent of <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>. The momentum was further increased by a growing movement for academic journal publishing reform, and with it gold and libre OA. Electronic publishing created new benefits as compared to paper publishing but beyond that, it contributed to causing problems in traditional publishing models.
</p><p>The premises behind open access publishing are that there are viable funding models to maintain traditional <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer review</a> standards of quality while also making the following changes:
</p>
<ul><li>Rather than making journal articles accessible through a <a href="/wiki/Subscription_business_model" title="Subscription business model">subscription business model</a>, all academic publications could be made free to read and published with some other cost-recovery model, such as publication charges, subsidies, or charging subscriptions only for the print edition, with the online edition <a href="/wiki/Gratis_versus_libre" title="Gratis versus libre">gratis</a> or "free to read".<sup id="cite_ref-Suber29-43_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber29-43-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Rather than applying traditional notions of <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> to academic publications, they could be <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/libre#Adjective" class="extiw" title="wikt:libre">libre</a> or "free to build upon".<sup id="cite_ref-Suber29-43_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber29-43-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>An obvious advantage of open access journals is the free access to scientific papers regardless of affiliation with a subscribing library and improved access for the general public; this is especially true in developing countries. Lower costs for research in academia and industry have been claimed in the <a href="/wiki/Budapest_Open_Access_Initiative" title="Budapest Open Access Initiative">Budapest Open Access Initiative</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> although others have argued that OA may raise the <a href="/wiki/Total_cost" title="Total cost">total cost</a> of publication,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> and further increase economic incentives for exploitation in academic publishing.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The open access movement is motivated by the problems of social inequality caused by restricting access to academic research, which favor large and wealthy institutions with the financial means to purchase access to many journals, as well as the economic challenges and perceived unsustainability of academic publishing.<sup id="cite_ref-Suber29-43_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber29-43-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stakeholders_and_concerned_communities">Stakeholders and concerned communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stakeholders and concerned communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The intended audience of research articles is usually other researchers. Open access helps researchers as readers by opening up access to articles that their libraries do not subscribe to. One of the great beneficiaries of open access may be users in <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>, where currently some <a href="/wiki/Universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Universities">universities</a> find it difficult to pay for subscriptions required to access the most recent journals.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Some schemes exist for providing subscription scientific publications to those affiliated to institutions in developing countries at little or no cost.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> All researchers benefit from open access as no library can afford to subscribe to every <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journal</a> and most can only afford a small fraction of them â€“ this is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Serials_crisis" title="Serials crisis">serials crisis</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Open access extends the reach of research beyond its immediate academic circle. An open access article can be read by anyone â€“ a <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professional</a> in the field, a <a href="/wiki/Researcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Researcher">researcher</a> in another field, a <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> or <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servant</a>, or an interested <a href="/wiki/Layperson" class="mw-redirect" title="Layperson">layperson</a>. Indeed, a 2008 study revealed that <a href="/wiki/Mental_health_professional" title="Mental health professional">mental health professionals</a> are roughly twice as likely to read a relevant article if it is freely available.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The main reason authors make their articles openly accessible is to maximize their <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">research impact</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> 
There have been claims of higher citation rates for open access authors.<sup id="cite_ref-Eysenbach_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eysenbach-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The overall citation rates for a time period of 2 years (2010â€“2011) were 30% higher for subscription journals, but, after controlling for discipline, journal age and publisher location, the differences largely disappeared in most subcategories, except for those launched prior to 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-BjÃ¶rk_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BjÃ¶rk-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> A study in 2001 first reported an open access <a href="/wiki/Citation_impact" title="Citation impact">citation impact</a> advantage,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two major studies dispute the claim that open access articles lead to more citations.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_2008_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis_2008-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> A randomized controlled trial of open access publishing involving 36 participating journals in the sciences, social sciences, and humanities found that open access articles (n=712) received significantly more downloads and reached a broader audience within the first year, yet were cited no more frequently, nor earlier, than subscription-access control articles (n=2533) within 3 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_2008_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis_2008-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many other studies, both major and minor and with varying degrees of methodological rigor, find that an open access article is more likely to be used and cited than one behind subscription barriers.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, a 2006 study in <i><a href="/wiki/PLoS_Biology" class="mw-redirect" title="PLoS Biology">PLoS Biology</a></i> found that articles published as immediate open access in <i><a href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences">PNAS</a></i> were three times more likely to be cited than non-open access papers, and were also cited more than <i>PNAS</i> articles that were only self-archived.<sup id="cite_ref-Eysenbach_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eysenbach-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> This result has been challenged as an artifact of authors self-selectively paying to publish their higher quality articles in hybrid open access journals,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> whereas a 2010 study found that the open access citation advantage was equally big whether self-archiving was self-selected or mandated.<sup id="cite_ref-Gargouri_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gargouri-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2010 study of 27,197 articles in 1,984 journals used institutionally mandated open access instead of randomized open access to control for bias on the part of authors toward self-selectively making their better (hence more citeable) articles open access. The result was a replication of the repeatedly reported open access citation advantage, with the advantage being equal in size and significance whether the open access was self-selected or mandated.<sup id="cite_ref-Gargouri_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gargouri-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2016 study reported that the odds of an open access journal being referenced on the English Wikipedia are 47% higher than for paywalled journals, and suggested that this constitutes a significant "amplifier" effect for science published on such platforms.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Scholars are paid by research funders and/or their universities to do research; the published article is the report of the work they have done, rather than an item for commercial gain. The more the article is used, cited, applied and built upon, the better for research as well as for the researcher's career.<sup id="cite_ref-uk_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Open access can reduce publication delays, an obstacle which led some research fields such as high-energy physics to adopt widespread preprint access.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some professional organizations have encouraged use of open access: in 2001, the <a href="/wiki/International_Mathematical_Union" title="International Mathematical Union">International Mathematical Union</a> communicated to its members that "Open access to the mathematical literature is an important goal" and encouraged them to "[make] available electronically as much of our own work as feasible" to "[enlarge] the reservoir of freely available primary mathematical material, particularly helping scientists working without adequate library access".<sup id="cite_ref-imu_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imu-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_funders_and_universities">Research funders and universities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Research funders and universities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="#Policies_and_mandates"> Â§&#160;Policies and mandates</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Open-access_mandate#Instances" title="Open-access mandate">Open-access mandate Â§&#160;Instances</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Research_funding" class="mw-redirect" title="Research funding">Research funding</a> agencies and universities want to ensure that the research they fund and support in various ways has the greatest possible research impact.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> As a means of achieving this, research funders are beginning to expect open access to the research they support. Many of them (including all seven UK Research Councils) have already adopted green open access <a href="/wiki/Self-archiving" title="Self-archiving">self-archiving</a> mandates, and others are on the way to do so (see <a href="/wiki/ROARMAP" class="mw-redirect" title="ROARMAP">ROARMAP</a>).
</p><p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/NIH_Public_Access_Policy" title="NIH Public Access Policy">NIH Public Access Policy</a>, an open access mandate was put into law, and required that research papers describing research funded by the National Institutes of Health must be available to the public free through PubMed Central within 12 months of publication.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Universities">Universities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Universities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A growing number of universities are providing institutional repositories in which their researchers can deposit their published articles. Some open access advocates believe that institutional repositories will play a very important role in responding to open access mandates from funders.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> EnablingOpenScholarship (EPS) provides universities with OA policy-building.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2005, 16 major <a href="/wiki/Dutch_universities" title="Dutch universities">Dutch universities</a> cooperatively launched <a href="/wiki/Darenet" title="Darenet">DAREnet</a>, the Digital Academic Repositories, making over 47,000 research papers available to anyone with internet access.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> From 1 January 2007, at the completion of the DARE programme, KNAW Research Information has taken over responsibility for the DAREnet portal. On 2 June 2008, DAREnet has been incorporated into the scholarly portal <a href="/wiki/NARCIS_(National_Academic_Research_and_Collaboration_Information_System)" class="mw-redirect" title="NARCIS (National Academic Research and Collaboration Information System)">NARCIS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> At the end of 2009, NARCIS provided access to 185,000 open access publications from all Dutch universities, KNAW, NWO and a number of scientific institutes.
</p><p>In 2011, a group of universities in North America formed the Coalition of Open Access Policy Institutions (COAPI).<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Starting with 21 institutions where the faculty had either established an open access policy or were in the process of implementing one, COAPI now has nearly 50 members. These institutions' administrators, faculty and librarians, and staff support the international work of the Coalition's awareness-raising and advocacy for open access. Members agree to the following COAPI Principles:
</p>
<ol><li>The immediate and barrier-free online dissemination of scholarly research resulting in faster growth of new knowledge, increased impact of research, and improved return on public research investments</li>
<li>Developing and implementing institutional open access policies</li>
<li>Sharing experiences and best practices in the development and implementation of Open Access Policies with individuals at institutions interested in cultivating cultures of open access</li>
<li>Fostering a more open scholarly communication system through cultural and legislative change at the local, national, and international levels<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>In 2012, the Harvard Open Access Project released its guide to good practices for university open-access policies,<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> focusing on rights-retention policies that allow universities to distribute faculty research without seeking permission from publishers.
</p><p>In 2013 a group of nine Australian universities formed the Australian Open Access Support Group (AOASG) to advocate, collaborate, raise awareness, and lead and build capacity in the open access space in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, the group expanded to include all eight New Zealand universities and was renamed the Australasian Open Access Support Group.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> It was then renamed the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://aoasg.org.au/">Australasian Open Access Strategy Group</a>, highlighting its emphasis on strategy. The awareness raising activities of the AOASG include presentations, workshops, blogs, and a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://aoasg.org.au/webinar-series-2017/">webinar series</a> on open access issues.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries_and_librarians">Libraries and librarians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Libraries and librarians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As information professionals, <a href="/wiki/Librarian" title="Librarian">librarians</a> are vocal and active advocates of open access. These librarians believe that open access promises to remove both the <i>price barriers</i> and the <i>permission barriers</i> that undermine library efforts to provide access to the scholarly record,<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> as well as helping to address the <a href="/wiki/Serials_crisis" title="Serials crisis">serials crisis</a>. Many library associations have either signed major open access declarations, or created their own. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Library_Association" title="Canadian Library Association">Canadian Library Association</a> endorsed a Resolution on Open Access in June 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Librarians also lead education and outreach initiatives to faculty, administrators, and others about the benefits of open access. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_College_and_Research_Libraries" title="Association of College and Research Libraries">Association of College and Research Libraries</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> has developed a Scholarly Communications Toolkit.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Research_Libraries" title="Association of Research Libraries">Association of Research Libraries</a> has documented the need for increased access to scholarly information, and was a leading founder of the <a href="/wiki/Scholarly_Publishing_and_Academic_Resources_Coalition" title="Scholarly Publishing and Academic Resources Coalition">Scholarly Publishing and Academic Resources Coalition</a> (SPARC).<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At most universities, the library manages the institutional repository, which provides free access to scholarly work by the university's faculty. The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Association_of_Research_Libraries" title="Canadian Association of Research Libraries">Canadian Association of Research Libraries</a> has a program<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> to develop institutional repositories at all Canadian university libraries.
</p><p>An increasing number of libraries provide hosting services for open access journals. A 2008 survey by the Association of Research Libraries<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> found that 65% of surveyed libraries either are <a href="/wiki/Library_publishing" title="Library publishing">involved in journal publishing</a>, or are planning to become involved in the very near future.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, open access activist <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Swartz" title="Aaron Swartz">Aaron Swartz</a> was posthumously awarded the American Library Association's <a href="/wiki/James_Madison_Award" title="James Madison Award">James Madison Award</a> for being an "outspoken advocate for public participation in government and unrestricted access to peer-reviewed scholarly articles".<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> In March 2013, the entire editorial board and the editor-in-chief of the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Library_Administration" title="Journal of Library Administration">Journal of Library Administration</a></i> resigned en masse, citing a dispute with the journal's publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> One board member wrote of a "crisis of conscience about publishing in a journal that was not open access" after the death of Aaron Swartz.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The pioneer of the open access movement in France and one of the first librarians to advocate the <a href="/wiki/Self-archiving" title="Self-archiving">self-archiving</a> approach to open access worldwide is HÃ©lÃ¨ne Bosc.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> Her work is described in her "15-year retrospective".<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public">Public</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Public">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Open access to scholarly research is argued to be important to the public for a number of reasons. One of the arguments for public access to the scholarly literature is that most of the research is paid for by taxpayers through <a href="/wiki/Government_grant" class="mw-redirect" title="Government grant">government grants</a>, who therefore have a right to access the results of what they have funded. This is one of the primary reasons for the creation of advocacy groups such as The Alliance for Taxpayer Access in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> Examples of people who might wish to read scholarly literature include individuals with medical conditions (or family members of such individuals) and serious hobbyists or 'amateur' scholars who may be interested in specialized scientific literature (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur astronomers</a>). Additionally, professionals in many fields may be interested in continuing education in the research literature of their field, and many businesses and academic institutions cannot afford to purchase articles from or subscriptions to much of the research literature that is published under a toll access model.
</p><p>Even those who do not read scholarly articles benefit indirectly from open access.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> For example, patients benefit when their doctor and other <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> professionals have access to the latest research. As argued by open access advocates, open access speeds research progress, productivity, and knowledge translation.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> Every researcher in the world can read an article, not just those whose library can afford to subscribe to the particular journal in which it appears. Faster discoveries benefit everyone. High school and <a href="/wiki/Junior_college" title="Junior college">junior college</a> students can gain the information literacy skills critical for the knowledge age. Critics of the various open access initiatives claim that there is little evidence that a significant amount of scientific literature is currently unavailable to those who would benefit from it.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> While no library has subscriptions to every journal that might be of benefit, virtually all published research can be acquired via <a href="/wiki/Interlibrary_loan" title="Interlibrary loan">interlibrary loan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> Note that interlibrary loan may take a day or weeks depending on the loaning library and whether they will scan and email, or mail the article. Open access online, by contrast is faster, often immediate, making it more suitable than interlibrary loan for fast-paced research.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-income_countries">Low-income countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Low-income countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In developing nations, open access archiving and publishing acquires a unique importance. Scientists, health care professionals, and institutions in developing nations often do not have the capital necessary to access scholarly literature, although schemes exist to give them access for little or no cost. Among the most important is <a href="/wiki/HINARI" title="HINARI">HINARI</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> the Health InterNetwork Access to Research Initiative, sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>. HINARI, however, also has restrictions. For example, individual researchers may not register as users unless their institution has access,<sup id="cite_ref-WHOeligibility_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHOeligibility-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> and several countries that one might expect to have access do not have access at all (not even "low-cost" access) (e.g. South Africa).<sup id="cite_ref-WHOeligibility_113-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHOeligibility-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many open access projects involve international collaboration. For example, the <a href="/wiki/SciELO" title="SciELO">SciELO</a> (Scientific Electronic Library Online),<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> is a comprehensive approach to full open access journal publishing, involving a number of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin American</a> countries. <a href="/wiki/Bioline_International" title="Bioline International">Bioline International</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organization</a> dedicated to helping publishers in developing countries is a collaboration of people in the UK, Canada, and Brazil; the Bioline International Software is used around the world. <a href="/wiki/Research_Papers_in_Economics" title="Research Papers in Economics">Research Papers in Economics</a> (RePEc), is a collaborative effort of over 100 volunteers in 45 countries. The <a href="/wiki/Public_Knowledge_Project" title="Public Knowledge Project">Public Knowledge Project</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source</a> publishing software <a href="/wiki/Open_Journal_Systems" title="Open Journal Systems">Open Journal Systems</a> (OJS), which is now in use around the world, for example by the <a href="/wiki/African_Journals_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="African Journals Online">African Journals Online</a> group, and one of the most active development groups is Portuguese. This international perspective has resulted in advocacy for the development of <a href="/wiki/Open-source_appropriate_technology" title="Open-source appropriate technology">open-source appropriate technology</a> and the necessary open access to relevant information for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Open_access&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main argument against open access, author's paid fee based journals, is the possible damage to the <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer review</a> system, diminishing the overall quality of scientific journal publishing. For example, in 2009, a hoax paper generated by a computer program was accepted for publication by a major publisher under the <a href="/wiki/Author-pays_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Author-pays model">author-pays-for-publication model</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> In a similar <a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Afraid_of_Peer_Review%3F" title="Who&#39;s Afraid of Peer Review?">incident</a>, a staff writer for Science magazine and popular science publications targeted the open access system in 2013 by submitting to some such journals a deeply flawed paper on the purported effect of a lichen constituent. About 60% of those journals, including journals published by the major academic publishers <a href="/wiki/Sage_Publications" class="mw-redirect" title="Sage Publications">Sage Publications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elsevier" title="Elsevier">Elsevier</a> the Journal of Natural Pharmaceuticals, accepted the faked medical paper. However, journals published by notable open access publishers <a href="/wiki/Public_Library_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Library of Science">PLOS</a>, <a href="/wiki/BioMed_Central" title="BioMed Central">BioMed Central</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hindawi_Publishing_Corporation" title="Hindawi Publishing Corporation">Hindawi Publishing Corporation</a> rejected the fake article. In addition, the faked paper was not published in subscription journals as a control.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup> As a result, this experiment was criticized for using a flawed methodology and not including a control group.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> Many newer open access journals also lack the reputation of their subscription counterparts, which have been in business for decades. This effect has been diminishing though since 2001, reflecting the emergence of high quality professional open access publishers such as <a href="/wiki/PLOS" title="PLOS">PLOS</a> and <a href="/wiki/BioMed_Central" title="BioMed Central">BioMed Central</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BjÃ¶rk_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BjÃ¶rk-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Opponents of the open access model continue to assert that the pay-for-access model is necessary to ensure that the publishers are adequately compensated for their work. Scholarly journal publishers that support pay-for-access claim that the "gatekeeper" role they play, maintaining a scholarly reputation, arranging for peer review, and editing and indexing articles, require economic resources that are not supplied under an open access model. Opponents claim that open access is not necessary to ensure fair access for developing nations; differential pricing or financial aid from developed countries or institutions can make access to proprietary journals affordable. Some critics also point out the lack of funding for author fees.<sup id="cite_ref-vogel_121-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vogel-121">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A study published in 2010 showed that roughly 20% of the total number of peer-reviewed articles published in 2008 could be found openly accessible.<sup id="cite_ref-doi10.1371/journal.pone.0011273_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi10.1371/journal.pone.0011273-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> Another study found that by 2010, 7.9% of all academic journals with <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factors</a> were gold open access journals and showed a broad distribution of Gold Open Access journals throughout academic disciplines.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup> 8.5% of the journal literature could be found free at the publishersâ€™ sites (gold open access), of which 62% in full open access journals, 14% in <a href="/wiki/Delayed_open_access_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Delayed open access journal">delayed-access</a> subscription journals, and 24% as individually open articles in otherwise subscription journals. For an additional 11.9% of the articles, open access full text copies were available via green open access in either subject-based repositories (43%), institutional repositories (24%) or on the home pages of the authors or their departments (33%). These copies were further classified into exact copies of the published article (38%), manuscripts as accepted for publishing (46%) or manuscripts as submitted (15%).<sup id="cite_ref-doi10.1371/journal.pone.0011273_122-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi10.1371/journal.pone.0011273-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2010 study, of all scientific fields chemistry had the lowest overall share of open access (13%), while Earth Sciences had the highest (33%). In medicine, biochemistry and chemistry gold publishing in open access journals was more common than author self-archiving. In all other fields self-archiving was more common.
</p><p>In 2009, there were approximately 4,800 active open access journals, publishing around 190,000 articles.<sup id="cite_ref-BjÃ¶rk2011_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BjÃ¶rk2011-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> As of October 2015, this had increased to over 10,000 open access journals listed in the <a href="/wiki/Directory_of_Open_Access_Journals" title="Directory of Open Access Journals">Directory of Open Access Journals</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> though this number has fallen to 9,500 in January 2017. A study of random journals from the <a href="/wiki/Citation_index" title="Citation index">citations indexes</a> AHSCI, SCI and SSCI in 2013 came to the result that 88% of the journals were closed access and 12% were open access.<sup id="cite_ref-fuchs2013_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fuchs2013-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2013, a study done for the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> reported that 50% of a random sample of all articles published in 2011 as indexed by <a href="/wiki/Scopus" title="Scopus">Scopus</a> were freely accessible online by the end of 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> A 2017 study by the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Society" title="Max Planck Society">Max Planck Society</a> put the share of gold access articles in pure open access journals at around 13 percent of total research papers.<sup id="cite_ref-Max_Planck_Gesellschaft_129-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Max_Planck_Gesellschaft-129">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Registry_of_Open_Access_Repositories" title="Registry of Open Access Repositories">Registry of Open Access Repositories</a> (ROAR) indexes the creation, location and growth of open access <a href="/wiki/Open_access_repository" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access repository">open access repositories</a> and their contents.<sup id="cite_ref-roar_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roar-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> As of December 2017, over 4,500 institutional and cross-institutional repositories have been registered in ROAR.<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">&#91;130&#93;</a></sup>
